Athletic Shorts questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best athletic shorts balance comfort, breathability, and the right length for your activity. For running, look for lightweight, moisture-wicking fabrics and a secure fit, while training shorts should offer a bit more durability and stretch. Pockets and liners are optional but can make or break your experience; consider your primary use and personal preference.

What’s the difference between running shorts and training shorts?

Running shorts are built for forward motion and airflow. They’re usually lighter, with a shorter inseam (often 3 to 7 inches) and a focus on preventing bounce or chafe. Many have a built-in brief or a partial liner, and pockets are usually small or sealed to keep a phone or key secure. Training shorts, on the other hand, are designed for multi-directional movement, squatting, lunging, stretching. They tend to have a bit more room in the leg, a higher waistband, and thicker fabric that can handle gym-floor abrasion. Deep pockets are common because you might carry a phone and a gym card. The trade-off is less breathability and a heavier feel on the run.

How long should your athletic shorts be?

There’s no universal right answer, but inseam length matters for both performance and style. A 5-inch inseam hits mid-thigh and is popular for runners who want full range of motion without extra fabric. It’s a sporty look but can feel too short if you’re self-conscious about your thighs. Seven- and 9-inch shorts sit above the knee or right at it. They offer more coverage and usually have roomier leg openings, which feel more secure for weightlifting or casual wear. Nine-inch shorts are often considered hybrid, they work for the gym and for errands. The best advice: try a couple lengths and wear them through a warm-weather workout to see what feels natural.

What fabrics work best for different activities?

For hot-weather running, look for lightweight polyester or a polyester-nylon blend with a matte finish, it wicks sweat fast and dries nearly instantly. Many of these have a subtle texture that prevents the fabric from sticking to skin. If you’re prone to chafing, avoid 100% cotton or anything with a rough mesh. For lifting or HIIT, you want a bit of spandex (5 to 12 percent) for stretch and recovery. Look for double-weave or “training” fabric that resists pilling and holds its shape after dozens of wash cycles. Some shorts incorporate a brushed interior for softness, though that can trap heat in humid gyms. If you want an all-purpose pair, a midweight polyester-spandex blend with a DWR (water-resistant) finish is versatile but costs a bit more.

Should you get lined or unlined shorts?

Lined shorts (with a built-in brief or compression layer) are the most popular choice for running, they eliminate the need for separate underwear and reduce chafing on long miles. The liner is usually a light mesh that breathes well, but it can feel restrictive if you prefer loose-fitting boxers. Unlined shorts give you the freedom to wear your own underwear or a separate compression base layer. They’re easier to wash and don’t add bulk, but you need to choose the right underwear, something moisture-wicking and with flat seams. If you mainly use shorts for casual wear or the gym, unlined is more flexible. For dedicated runners logging double-digit miles, lined often wins for convenience.

What makes a good waistband for athletic shorts?

The best waistbands combine an internal drawstring with a wide, soft elastic at the back. A flat-front elastic that is thick enough to stay put but not so tight it digs in is key. Many premium shorts use a hidden drawstring that exits through a small slit, which looks cleaner and doesn’t bunch under a shirt. For running, a low-profile waistband that sits at your natural waist without rolling over is ideal. For training, a slightly higher rise that stays put during squats and deadlifts is better. Some shorts add a silicone grip on the inner waistband to keep shirts tucked, useful for the gym but unnecessary for most runners.

How do pockets affect performance?

Pockets vary widely, and the wrong design can ruin a run. The best running shorts use a flat, zippered pocket on the back or right hip, it holds a key or card without flopping. Some side pockets are slit-style and barely deep enough for a phone, while others are integrated into the liner with a compression fit that keeps everything tight against your thigh. For gym shorts, deep open pockets are fine, but check that they lie flat when empty. Mesh pockets are breathable but can tear more easily; polyester lining is stronger. If you’re tired of keys jingling or a phone bouncing, look for any pocket that closes securely, even a button or Velcro tab helps. Remember: more pockets usually mean more weight and potential chafe points, so decide what you really need to carry.

Frequently asked questions

What is the best inseam for preventing chafing?

Chafing is less about inseam length and more about the leg opening and fabric. A longer inseam (7 to 9 inches) often reduces skin-on-skin friction because the shorts cover more of the inner thigh, but the right answer is to choose a shorts with a smooth, flat-locked seam and a slightly snug fit at the hem.

Can you wear athletic shorts for casual everyday use?

Absolutely, as long as they aren’t too technical looking. Plain, mid-weight shorts in neutral colors with a 7- or 9-inch inseam and no reflective accents cross over well from gym to street. Avoid obvious running features like a built-in liner or a rear zipper pocket with a cord loop if you want them to look casual.

How often should you replace athletic shorts?

When the fabric pills heavily, the elastic waistband no longer stays snug, or the liner loses its shape, it’s time to replace them, usually after 1 to 3 years of regular use, depending on wash frequency and dryer heat. Air drying and washing on cold can extend their life significantly.

What’s the difference between compression shorts and loose shorts?

Compression shorts are tight-fitting and designed to support muscles and reduce vibration during high-impact activity. Loose shorts offer more airflow and freedom of movement but provide no muscle support. Some runners split the difference with a loose outer short over a tight compression liner.

Are double-layer shorts worth it?

They can be, especially for long runs or hot weather. The inner layer (usually a mesh brief) wicks sweat and reduces chafe, while the outer shell stays dry and non-restrictive. The downside is slightly more laundry and a higher price, but many experienced runners swear by them for comfort on half-marathon distances and beyond.

How do I stop my shorts from riding up?

A secure waistband and a leg opening that fits snugly without being tight are key. Look for shorts with an elastic or silicone grip at the leg hem, which many running short have. If you wear a separate base layer, compression shorts underneath can hold everything in place.

In shortFinding the right athletic shorts really comes down to matching the fabric, length, and features to how you actually move. Trust your own comfort over any trend, if a pair feels great for a mile and still feels great after you wash it, you’ve found your winner. Don’t be afraid to try a couple of different styles until the right one clicks.
